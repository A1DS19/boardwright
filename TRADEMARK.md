# Boardwright trademark policy

The Boardwright **source code** is licensed under MIT (see [LICENSE](LICENSE)) and you can do almost anything with it: use it, fork it, modify it, redistribute it, sell services around it.

This document covers something different: the **name and brand** — "Boardwright", "Boardwright Studio", the wordmark, and any associated logos. These are trademarks of Jose Padilla and are not licensed under MIT.

The goal of this policy is simple: if a user installs something called "Boardwright", they should be getting the real Boardwright, with the real maintainers behind it. We are *not* trying to stop forks, modifications, or commercial use — we are only protecting the name so users aren't confused about what they're getting.

## What you can do without asking

- **Fork the code** to your own repository. Modify it. Run it. Build on it.
- **Redistribute** the source code under MIT, including any modifications you make.
- **Use it commercially** — internally at your company, in your client work, in products you sell.
- **Make accurate descriptive references** to Boardwright. Examples that are fine:
  - "This is a fork of Boardwright."
  - "Compatible with Boardwright."
  - "Built using Boardwright."
  - "We use Boardwright internally to design our boards."
  - Blog posts, tutorials, comparison articles, and reviews — favorable or critical.
- **Use the unmodified Boardwright name** in tool outputs, error messages, and documentation **of an unmodified or trivially-modified Boardwright distribution**.

## What requires permission

Before doing any of the following, please open an issue or email — we are usually happy to grant permission, but we want to know about it:

- **Distributing a modified version of Boardwright under the name "Boardwright"** or any name that includes "Boardwright" (e.g. "Boardwright Pro", "Boardwright Cloud", "Boardwright for X").
- **Selling a product or service** whose primary identifier is the Boardwright name or logo (e.g. "BoardwrightHQ", "boardwright-as-a-service.com").
- **Using the Boardwright logo or visual identity** (the PCB-trace-green wordmark, brand glow, or other visual marks from the official site or distribution) on your own product, marketing materials, or website in a way that suggests endorsement, affiliation, or that your product *is* Boardwright.
- **Using a domain name** that includes "boardwright" for a product page, service, or company that is not the official project (informational fan sites and personal blogs are fine).
- **Trademark applications** anywhere in the world covering "Boardwright" or any confusingly similar name in software, EDA, or PCB-design categories.

## What is not OK

- Forking Boardwright, **renaming** it minimally (e.g. "Boardwryte", "Boardrite", "BoardWrite"), and selling it commercially. The point of this rule is to prevent bad-faith confusion — if a user could reasonably mistake your product for Boardwright, that's a problem regardless of spelling.
- Pretending your fork **is** Boardwright when it has been substantively modified.
- Using Boardwright branding to imply **endorsement** or **partnership** that doesn't exist.

## How to ask for permission

Open an issue at https://github.com/A1DS19/boardwright/issues with the subject `[trademark] <what you want to do>`, or email the maintainer (see the GitHub profile). We try to respond within a week. We grant permission generously when the request is in good faith.

## Why this exists

Open-source code lives or dies by its name. If anyone could ship a different product under the name "Boardwright," users would have no way to know which version they're installing, which maintainers stand behind it, or whether issues they hit are real bugs or someone else's fork. Trademark protects the user, not just the project.

This policy is intentionally narrow: we restrict the **name and brand**, not the code. Take the code, modify it, sell services with it, fork it forever — that's MIT, and that's by design. Just don't call your fork Boardwright.

## Inspiration

This policy is modelled on how PostgreSQL, Linux, KiCad, Python, and React handle their trademarks. None of those projects let people fork, rename minimally, and sell the result; all of them let anyone fork and ship under a clearly different name. We're doing the same thing.
